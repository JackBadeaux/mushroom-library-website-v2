Guides:

Importance of Sterile Technique

  • You have to pressure cook grains, bulk substrate, and agar at 15psi and 30min to 3hours depending on what is sterilizing. (pasturizing is used for manure/compost and straw bulk substrates)
  • Sterilization kills everything, including spores — essential for agar and grains. Pasteurization only reduces harmful organisms and is used for straw or manure/compost substrates.
  • Flame sterilizing is very important and is when you heat needles and scaples if you are reusing them. They are sterile out the package however
  • Prevents contamination from mold, bacteria, and unwanted fungi that can ruin entire grows.
  • Improves success rate with liquid cultures, grain spawn, and agar transfers.
  • Extends the life and viability of cultures by avoiding hidden pathogens early on.
  • Essential for producing clean spore prints, syringes, and reliable genetics for future grows.
  • Always work in a still air box (SAB) or in front of a laminar flow hood when doing sterile transfers or inoculations.
  • Wipe down surfaces, gloves, and tools with 70% isopropyl alcohol regularly during your work session.
  • Air is full of microscopic spores and bacteria — avoid drafts, sudden movements, and speaking while doing sterile work.

Beginner Cultivation Steps

  • Start with a clean and organized workspace to reduce chances of contamination.
  • Choose your species — oysters are fast and forgiving, lion's mane and reishi are great for medicinal use.
  • Inoculate sterilized grain jars or bags using a clean liquid culture or spore syringe.
  • Allow the grain to fully colonize in a dark or dim environment around 70-75°F (21-24°C).
  • Once fully colonized, transfer to bulk substrate (Master's Mix, coco coir, etc.) in a monotub or grow bag.
  • Wait for full colonization again — then introduce fruiting conditions: high humidity, fresh air, and indirect light.
  • Harvest when caps flatten or just before spores drop, depending on species.
  • Dry your mushrooms using a dehydrator or fan, and store them in airtight containers with desiccant packs.

Agar Work

  • Use sterilized petri dishes or small jars filled with nutrient agar (like MEA or PDA).
  • Flame sterilize your scalpel before every cut to prevent introducing contaminants.
  • Work inside a still air box or in front of a flow hood to keep the environment sterile.
  • Transfer clean mycelium from spores, tissue, or colonized grain to new agar plates.
  • Label your plates with the strain, transfer number, and date to track progress.
  • Watch for fast-growing, rhizomorphic (stringy) growth — that's usually what you want to clone or expand.
  • Discard plates with contamination, or isolate clean sections away from it quickly.

Monotub Setup

  • Use a clear plastic tub with a lid and drill holes along the sides for passive air exchange.
  • Fill the tub with a 2-3 inch layer of colonized grain mixed into bulk substrate.
  • Keep the lid on during colonization (tape over holes if needed for higher CO₂).
  • After colonization, remove tape or stuff holes with polyfill to allow FAE (fresh air exchange).
  • Mist and fan lightly if surface looks dry or air looks stagnant — aim for a glistening surface.
  • Maintain temps around 70-75°F (21-24°C) and ambient indirect light for healthy pinning.
  • Harvest mushrooms just before or at cap flattening to avoid excessive spore drops.

How to Make a Spore Print

  • Harvest a mature mushroom with the cap fully open but before heavy spore drop.
  • Cut the stem as close to the cap as possible with a sterile scalpel or blade.
  • Place the cap, gill-side down, onto sterile foil or white paper (foil stores better).
  • Cover the cap with a clean glass or bowl to prevent airflow and contamination.
  • Leave for 12-24 hours in a still, clean environment — spores will drop and form a print.
  • Gently remove the cap, then fold or store the print in a small baggie or foil square.
  • Label the print with strain name and date, and store in a cool, dry place.

How to Do a Dual Extraction

  • Dual extraction is used to extract both water-soluble (polysaccharides) and alcohol-soluble (triterpenes) compounds from medicinal mushrooms like Reishi and Lion's Mane.
  • First, do a hot water extraction: simmer dried mushrooms in water for 2-3 hours, then strain and save the liquid.
  • Next, do an alcohol extraction: soak the same mushrooms in high-proof alcohol (e.g. 80-95%) for 2-4 weeks in a sealed jar, shaking daily.
  • After the alcohol extraction is complete, strain the liquid and discard the solids.
  • Combine the water extract and alcohol extract in your desired ratio (often 1:1 or 2:1 water:alcohol).
  • Store the final tincture in a dark glass dropper bottle, labeled with the date and mushroom species.
  • Shake before use. The tincture can last over a year when stored in a cool, dark place.
  • Used properly, dual extraction can concentrate the full spectrum of a mushroom's medicinal benefits.
